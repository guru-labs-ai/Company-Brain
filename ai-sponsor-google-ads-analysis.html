Can we actually run Google Ads?

Checked against Google's own written policy and live advertiser data, not guesswork.
July 20, 2026 · Mariam

The short version

The empty search results aren't an open field — they're a restricted category. Google treats drug-and-alcohol recovery support as a certified vertical, the same way it treats pharmacies. The reason you saw no ads is not that nobody thought of it; it's that most people aren't allowed to be there.

The one type of advertiser that does show up is addiction treatment centres who paid to get certified — and they're running hundreds of ads.

This doesn't kill Google for us. But it changes what we'd be buying, and there are three routes that sit outside the restriction entirely. Details below — and everything here is checkable in about a minute on your phone.

What Google actually says
Google Ads · Addiction services policy
"Clinical addiction treatment providers, Recovery support services like sober living environments and mutual help organizations, Lead generators or referral agencies for addiction services, Crisis hotlines for drug and alcohol addiction"

That's the list of what requires certification before an ad can run. AI Sponsor is built entirely around AA, NA and the 12 steps — so there's a real chance Google's reviewers read us as sitting inside that second line.

Where it applies

Certification is required in the United States, Canada, Australia, France, Ireland and New Zealand — which is essentially the entire market we'd want to advertise into.

The part that matters most: we may not be able to buy our way in

Google's certification runs through a third party called LegitScript. Their addiction-treatment certification covers licensed clinical providers — and their published scope explicitly excludes recovery apps, AI chatbots, mobile applications and peer-support platforms.

So if Google classifies us as in scope, we'd need a certificate we aren't eligible to buy. Blocked, with no route to unblock. If it ever did apply, it runs somewhere around $1,500 up front plus $2,500–3,000 a year — but the published figures vary and I'd confirm them directly rather than plan on them.

Being straight about what that does and doesn't prove

There are two explanations for those blanks, and I can't yet tell them apart. Either they're restricted from running, or they simply chose other channels — consumer subscription apps often buy through Apple Search Ads and Meta instead, which would be a normal decision.

Either way the useful conclusion is the same: nobody in our category is proving Google works. We would be first, not late.

What I could not confirm

Open questions I'm not going to paper over

  • Whether Google would actually reject us. The policy language could cover us. It hasn't been tested against our specific product, and reviewers apply these things inconsistently.
  • Real search volume and click costs. This is the number you actually want and I don't have it. Keyword Planner needs a Google Ads account, and I couldn't get into one. I also don't know whether we have an account at all — your $300K of ads experience is Meta.
  • Why the recovery apps show no ads. Blocked or by choice — unresolved.
  • I tried Google Trends as a substitute and threw the result away. "AI sponsor" shows a steep rise, which looks like great news, but the top regions are Washington DC and Delaware — that's corporate and legislative sponsorship, not AA sponsorship. It's measuring the AI boom, not us. Same trap as the word "sponsor" causing confusion elsewhere.
Where the openings are
1 · The half of our product the restriction doesn't touch
Google explicitly puts "impulse control disorders, behavioral addiction, or nicotine addiction" out of scope. So Al-Anon and family members, GA, DA, SLAA and OA all sit outside the restricted category entirely.
That's an advertisable product hiding inside a restricted one — and it points at the family angle we already identified as an underserved market with money and urgency.
2 · The chips and medallions searches
Commercial intent, live merchants, cheap clicks, and no addiction-services restriction on a physical keepsake. This is your instinct from the call and it holds up — but only if we build the chip offer to meet it. On its own it sends buyers to a subscription they didn't ask for.
3 · Organic search, which no ad policy governs
The restriction applies to paid placement. Nothing stops us ranking. And "Is there an AI sponsor app?" is a question Google is already surfacing with no clear owner.
Our site currently has two indexable pages. That's the real constraint on this route, and it's fixable by us without permission from anyone.
What I'd do
Recommendation

Don't take this document's word for it either — test it for nothing. Set up the Google Ads account, build one campaign, submit the ads, and don't turn on spend. Approval or rejection comes back within a couple of days and costs zero. That answers definitively what a policy page can only suggest.

If the ads clear, we get real volume and click-cost numbers out of Keyword Planner the same day and we can size the channel properly.

If they're rejected, we've lost nothing, and we already know where to go: the family and behavioural side that's explicitly out of scope, and organic.

What I need from you: confirmation of whether a Google Ads account exists, and access to it if it does.

The thing worth holding on to

Your read on intent was right — someone searching "sober sponsor app" is worth far more than someone scrolling past an ad. That instinct isn't wrong. What's different from the assumption is why the field looks empty: it's fenced, not unclaimed.

And the fence has a real upside. If we do get through it, almost nobody else in our category is there.
